USC alumni research interview sequence

Automated outreach → book a 20-minute research interview

Campaign setup
Conversion goal

Book a 20-minute research interview with a USC-connected professional in private equity, M&A diligence, or financial services, to learn how they organize and manage deal data.

Entry trigger — Day 0 starts

A contact is added to the outreach segment — the outreach-list equivalent of a "signup." In a marketing-automation platform this is the enrollment trigger: enter the sequence, tag usc-finance-interview, start Day 0.

The governing rule
Reply detection exits the sequence

The instant a contact replies, they leave the automated flow and a person takes over scheduling. No lead should ever receive an automated "just bumping this" email after they've already answered — it's the fastest way to look like a bot and lose a warm alum.

Every send in this sequence carries the implicit condition: only if no reply has been received.

Two positive off-ramps

Booking via the scheduling link exits the sequence entirely.

Clicking the link but not finishing the booking routes to a short booking-recovery note (Email A) rather than the next nurture email.

Micro-personas
The Generous Mentor

Senior alum (Partner / MD / Principal). Time-poor but emotionally motivated to give back and mentor students. Moved by Trojan identity, legacy, and being thanked rather than sold to.

Targeted byEmail 1's "fellow Trojan" open, and Email 4, which thanks them for what they do for the USC community and leaves the door open with zero guilt.

The Busy Operator

Mid-to-senior alum (VP / Director). Slammed, protective of time, and instinctively reads outreach as a sales trap. Needs specificity, brevity, the explicit "not pitching," and a one-tap path.

Targeted byEmail 1's low-friction framing, Email 2 (respect for time, the 15-minute flex, top-of-inbox), and the scheduling link throughout.

The Curious Peer

Junior alum (Associate / Senior Associate). Recently a student, intellectually engaged, relates to the research itself. Won't move for "help a student," but will move for an interesting idea.

Targeted byEmail 3, which leads with the substantive finding, shrinks the ask to one question, and offers to share the group's results — a peer-to-peer exchange rather than a favor.

Why this covers everyone

Each persona is reached by a different email, so a single sequence covers the whole recipient spectrum without segmenting the list up front — the branching and the shifting objective of each send do the segmentation for you.

Note from the source doc: if your course requires your own validated micro-personas, swap these in — the sequence structure maps cleanly onto whichever set you're graded against.

Strategy rationale
Escalating delays (0 → 4 → 9 → 16)

Early follow-ups are tight because inbox attention decays fast; later ones widen because someone who hasn't replied by Day 9 needs space, not pressure. The cadence reads as persistent-but-respectful rather than needy.

Each email changes its job, not just its wording

Email 1 asks. Email 2 fights for visibility (and adapts on open data). Email 3 adds value and shrinks the ask to a single question. Email 4 releases pressure and banks goodwill.

A sequence that just re-sends the same request four times trains people to ignore it.

Behavior drives the branches

Opens decide Email 2's subject strategy. Clicks route to booking recovery instead of more nurture. Replies exit the flow entirely.

The automation is there to be invisible — the recipient should feel like a person is thoughtfully following up, never like they're inside a drip campaign.

Objective progression

Reply/book → reply → engagement (reply or click) → final reply/exit.

Micro-conversions (an open, a click, a one-line answer) are treated as wins, because each one is a signal to re-route rather than keep pushing.
